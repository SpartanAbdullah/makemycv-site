If you have applied to UAE jobs and heard nothing back despite being qualified, there is a good chance a piece of software, not a person, made that decision. Job seekers often ask whether Dubai and the wider UAE actually use applicant tracking systems. The short answer is yes — widely. Here is how it works and how to make sure your CV survives it.

## Yes — and More Than You'd Think

An **applicant tracking system (ATS)** is software that collects, parses, stores, and ranks job applications. In the UAE it is the norm rather than the exception, especially among the employers who attract the most applicants:

- **Large corporations and banks**, particularly across DIFC and ADGM
- **Multinationals** running global HR platforms like Workday, SuccessFactors, or Taleo
- **Hospitality and aviation groups** processing thousands of applications a month
- **Recruitment agencies**, which feed many UAE roles and run their own candidate databases
- **Government and semi-government** entities with formal e-recruitment portals

When a Dubai posting collects several hundred CVs in its first two days, no team reads them all. The ATS does the first cut, and only the CVs it ranks highly reach a human.

## How the ATS Decides

The software does two things. First, it **parses** your CV — pulling your name, contact details, work history, and skills into structured fields. If your layout confuses the parser, your information lands in the wrong place or vanishes. Second, it **ranks** you, largely by matching the keywords in your CV against the job description.

So there are two ways to fail the ATS: a layout it cannot read, or content that does not match the role. Both are fixable.

## What Breaks Your CV in an ATS

- **Multi-column layouts.** The parser reads left to right and scrambles two-column designs. This is the single most common cause of a mangled parse.
- **Text inside images or graphics.** If your name or headings are part of an image, the ATS cannot read them. Everything must be selectable text.
- **Tables, text boxes, and headers/footers.** Many older systems skip content placed in these.
- **Unusual fonts and creative section names.** "My Journey" instead of "Work Experience" can stop the parser recognising the section.
- **Inconsistent or non-standard date formats.** These confuse the timeline the ATS builds.

## How to Get Past It

1. **Use a single-column layout** with standard section headings: Summary, Work Experience, Education, Skills.
2. **Keep everything as real text** — no text baked into graphics.
3. **Match the job description's keywords** — the exact tools, certifications, and seniority terms, where they are true of you.
4. **Use consistent dates** in one clear format throughout.
5. **Save as a text-based PDF**, not a scanned or exported image.

## Does Beating the ATS Mean an Ugly CV?

No. An ATS-friendly CV and a recruiter-friendly CV are the same document: clean, single-column, and easy to scan. The myth that you must choose between "looks good" and "parses well" comes from designed templates that sacrifice readability for decoration. A well-built UAE template does both — which is also what survives the [seven-second human scan](/blog/7-seconds-thats-it) after the software passes you through.
