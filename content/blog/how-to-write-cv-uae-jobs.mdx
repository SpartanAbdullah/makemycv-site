If you are applying for jobs in the UAE, the CV that worked in your home country is probably costing you interviews. The Gulf market has its own conventions — visa status, a photo, attested qualifications — and its own brutal filter: recruiters spend roughly seven seconds on the first pass, and an applicant tracking system often screens you out before a human reads a word.

This guide walks through how to write a CV for UAE jobs from a blank page: the structure recruiters expect, what goes in each section, and the mistakes that quietly sink strong candidates. If you only need the layout rules, the [UAE CV format guide](/blog/cv-format-uae-2026) covers the structure in detail — this piece is about the writing itself.

## Start With Who Reads It

Before you type a single line, picture the two readers your CV must satisfy in order.

First is the **ATS** — the software that parses, stores, and ranks applications. It cannot see design. It reads text, headings, and dates. If your layout confuses it, your CV is filtered before any person sees it. We break down exactly how this works in the [ATS CV checklist](/blog/ats-cv-checklist-uae).

Second is the **human recruiter**, who scans the surviving CVs at speed. As we explain in [how UAE recruiters actually judge your CV](/blog/7-seconds-thats-it), the layout, your most recent job title, and your visa status carry more weight in those seconds than anything buried lower down.

Writing a UAE CV well means writing for both: clean enough for the software, sharp enough for the seven-second scan.

## Step 1 — Get the Structure Right

A UAE CV should run one to two pages, single column, in a standard font. Use this order, which both the ATS and the recruiter expect:

1. Header (name, title, contact, visa status)
2. Professional summary
3. Work experience
4. Education (with attestation note if relevant)
5. Skills
6. Languages
7. Certifications

Resist the multi-column, heavily designed templates from generic builders. They look impressive in preview and get shredded by the parser.

## Step 2 — Write a Header That Answers the Visa Question

Your header carries your name, a professional title, and your contact details — phone with the correct country code, a professional email, your city, and a LinkedIn URL. In the UAE, it also needs one line most Western CVs omit: your **visa status**.

UAE recruiters need to know whether hiring you means transferring an employment visa, cancelling an existing one, or sponsoring from scratch. State it plainly near the top: "UAE Residence Visa (Employment) — Transferable" or "On Visit Visa — Available Immediately." Leaving recruiters to guess is a common reason CVs get set aside.

A small professional photo is also normal in the UAE — expected in client-facing, hospitality, banking, and real-estate roles. It is optional, but it does not carry the discrimination concerns it would in the UK or Canada. We cover the decision in [should you put a photo on your UAE CV](/blog/photo-on-uae-cv).

## Step 3 — Write a Summary That Earns the Next Ten Seconds

Your professional summary is three sentences, sitting directly under the header. It is the first prose a recruiter reads, so it has to work hard.

The first sentence names your role and years of experience. The second names your industry and geographic context. The third names your defining skill or most recent accomplishment, with a number if you have one. Skip the filler — "results-driven team player" tells a recruiter nothing, and they skim past it automatically.

For a bank of ready-to-adapt examples across industries, see [professional summary examples for UAE CVs](/blog/professional-summary-examples-uae-cv).

## Step 4 — Turn Duties Into Achievements

Work experience is where most CVs go flat. The fix is simple: stop listing duties, start showing results.

A duty says *"Responsible for managing the procurement team."* An achievement says *"Led a 6-person procurement team and cut material lead times 25% across 2024."* Same job — but the second version gives the recruiter a reason to keep reading.

For each role, give the company, your title, dates, and location, followed by three to five bullets. Start every bullet with a verb and include a number wherever you honestly can: team size, revenue, percentage growth, budget, customer count. Both the ATS and the human reader weight quantified results.

If you are arriving from outside the Gulf, your previous employers' names mean little to a UAE hiring manager. Translate scope rather than relying on brand recognition — team size, regulatory environment, languages, and tools travel across borders; company names usually do not. Our [expat CV guide](/blog/expat-cv-uae-guide) goes deeper on this.

## Step 5 — Education, Attestation, and Skills

List your degree, university, country, and dates. In the UAE, many employers require **attested** qualifications for mid-to-senior roles, so if your degree is attested through the Ministry of Foreign Affairs, note it: "Bachelor of Commerce — University of Mumbai (Attested for UAE)." It removes a question from the screening conversation.

Keep skills as a clean, comma-separated list of genuine tools and competencies — the keywords the ATS matches against the job description. List languages with an honest proficiency level (Native, Fluent, Conversational), which matters more in the multilingual Gulf market than almost anywhere else.

## Step 6 — Tailor Before You Send

The single highest-return habit is tailoring. Read the job description, note the exact tools, certifications, and seniority terms it names, and make sure the same phrasing appears in your CV where it is true. The keyword-matching half of the ATS rewards a five-minute customisation per application far more than a generic CV blasted to fifty postings.

## If You Have No Experience Yet

Freshers and recent graduates are not stuck. You lead with education, projects, internships, and transferable skills rather than an empty work-history section, and you write a summary built around potential and relevant coursework.
