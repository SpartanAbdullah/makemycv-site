In the UAE, a CV and a resume are the same document. The country inherited the British term — "CV" — but the document UAE employers expect is what Americans would call a resume: one to two pages, reverse-chronological, focused on experience and skills. There is no situation in UAE corporate hiring where you need the long academic-style CV unless you are applying to a university or research institute.

That's the answer. Here's the context that makes it useful.

---

## Why the Confusion Exists

The two terms come from two markets that define them differently:

- **In the US and Canada:** a *resume* is the short 1–2 page document everyone sends to jobs. A *CV* (curriculum vitae) is a long academic document — every publication, conference, grant, and teaching post, often 5–15 pages.
- **In the UK, Europe, and the Commonwealth:** *CV* is simply the word for the short document. The word "resume" is rarely used.

The UAE, with its British administrative heritage and globally mixed workforce, uses **"CV" as the default word for the short document**. Job portals (Bayt, LinkedIn UAE, NaukriGulf, government careers pages) say CV. Recruiters say CV. But every recruiter in Dubai also understands "resume" — nobody is rejecting your application over vocabulary.

## What UAE Employers Actually Expect

Whatever you call it, the document itself should follow UAE conventions, which sit somewhere between British structure and Gulf-specific content:

| Element | US Resume | UK CV | UAE CV |
|---|---|---|---|
| Length | 1 page preferred | 2 pages | 1–2 pages |
| Photo | Never | Rare | Optional, common |
| Visa status | No | No | **Yes — near the top** |
| Nationality | Never | No | Expected |
| Languages | Optional | Optional | **Always include** |
| Format | ATS-safe | ATS-safe | ATS-safe, single column |

## The One Case Where "CV" Means Something Longer

If you are applying to a university, hospital research department, or research institute in the UAE, "CV" may mean the full academic document: publications, conference papers, grants, supervision history. The job posting will make this obvious — academic postings ask for research statements and references alongside it. For every other UAE employer, two pages is the ceiling.

## Practical Guidance

1. **Use the word "CV"** in your cover letters, emails, and LinkedIn headline when targeting UAE employers — it matches the local vocabulary.
2. **Build the document to UAE conventions**, not US or UK ones. A pasted-in American resume is missing visa status, nationality, and languages — three fields UAE recruiters scan for in the first seconds, as covered in [7 Seconds. That's It.](/blog/7-seconds-thats-it)
3. **Keep it parseable.** Whatever the name, the document goes through ATS software at most established UAE employers. Single column, standard headings, no tables — the [ATS checklist](/blog/ats-cv-checklist-uae) has the complete rules.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Are a resume and a CV the same in the UAE?

In practice, yes. In the UAE both words refer to the same document: a 1–2 page summary of your experience, skills, and education. "CV" is the more common term in UAE job postings, but recruiters use both interchangeably. The academic "full CV" (5+ pages of publications) exists only in university and research hiring.

### Is a CV accepted in Dubai?

Yes — "CV" is the standard term in Dubai and across the UAE. Job postings, recruiters, and government portals all ask for a CV. Send a 1–2 page document with UAE conventions: visa status, nationality, optional photo, and an ATS-safe layout.

### Does the US use a CV or a resume?

The US uses "resume" for the standard 1–2 page job application document and reserves "CV" for long academic documents listing publications and research. The UAE does not make this distinction — CV means the short document.

### Which is more professional, a CV or a resume?

Neither — they are the same document under two names. What reads as professional in the UAE is the content: a clean ATS-safe layout, stated visa status, measurable achievements, and a maximum of two pages.
